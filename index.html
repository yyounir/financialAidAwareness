Why Financial Aid Still Matters

Free tuition programs sound great — but they don't remove the need for financial aid. Here's why.

Short answer

"Free tuition" usually covers only tuition for certain students or institutions. Many costs and gaps remain — housing, food, books, transportation, part-time students, graduate programs, and eligibility restrictions — so financial aid still plays a critical role.

Where free tuition falls short

  • Non-tuition costs: Room, board, textbooks, supplies, and commuting can exceed tuition.
  • Eligibility limits: Programs may be income-tested, limited by residency, or restricted to specific institutions.
  • Part-time & returning students: Many free-tuition offers require full-time enrollment or continuous attendance.
  • Graduate & professional programs: Free tuition typically applies to undergraduate studies only.
  • Support services: Counseling, childcare, healthcare, and emergency aid are outside tuition coverage.
  • Funding volatility: Programs can change or be reduced during budget cuts or political shifts.

How financial aid fills the gaps

Financial aid combines grants, loans, work-study, and targeted supports to address the varied needs students have beyond tuition. Aid helps:

  1. Cover living and academic expenses.
  2. Support non-traditional students and those with part-time schedules.
  3. Provide emergency funds for unexpected crises.
  4. Assist students who aren’t covered by specific free-tuition policies.

Real-world examples

Even where state tuition is free for residents, many students still need aid for housing, childcare, or books. Community college programs may be free, but transfer students face hurdles that financial aid helps bridge.

What you can do

  • Understand the specifics of any "free tuition" program before assuming it covers everything.
  • Apply for grants and targeted aid early — many have deadlines separate from admissions.
  • Advocate for comprehensive student support that includes living costs and services.
